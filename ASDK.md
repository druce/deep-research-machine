## Extended Profile

### History

Autodesk, Inc. (NASDAQ: ADSK) traces its origins to April 1982, when John Walker, Daniel Drake, and 14 other programmers pooled $60,000 to form a software company in Sausalito, California. The founders were initially uncertain which product to pursue, having planned as many as five different applications. The question was settled at the 1982 Comdex trade show in Las Vegas, where strong market reception for their CAD product, and limited interest in their text editor, focused the company squarely on computer-aided design. AutoCAD shipped in December 1982 and generated $1.4 million in its first year. Walker later noted that Autodesk was profitable from its first month and never needed loans or outside investors.

The company went public in 1985. By 1989, annual sales had surpassed $100 million. Under CEO Carol Bartz in the early 1990s, Autodesk shed its diversification efforts and committed fully to design software. The acquisition of Softdesk in 1997 launched a push into specialized verticals — architecture, civil engineering, and manufacturing. Two deals in the 2000s proved transformative: the $133 million purchase of Revit Technology Corporation in 2002, which gave Autodesk its entry into Building Information Modeling (BIM), and the 2006 acquisition of Alias, which brought Maya, 3ds Max, and other 3D graphics tools to the media and entertainment portfolio.

Autodesk executed a full transition from perpetual licenses to subscription-based pricing beginning in 2016, a shift that temporarily pressured revenue but built the foundation for the recurring revenue model that now generates approximately 97% of the company's sales as of FY2026. The company relocated its headquarters from San Rafael to San Francisco in 2022. More recently, Autodesk restructured its workforce twice — cutting approximately 1,350 positions (roughly 9% of headcount) in 2025 and an additional approximately 1,000 positions (roughly 7% of the remaining workforce) in January 2026, totaling approximately 2,350 reductions over 13 months — as it redirects investment toward AI and cloud computing.

### Core Businesses, Products, and Services

Autodesk, Inc. (NASDAQ: ADSK) is a global leader in 3D design, engineering, and entertainment software, serving professionals across architecture, engineering, construction and operations (AECO), manufacturing, and media and entertainment (M&E). Founded in 1982, the company revolutionized computer-aided design by bringing CAD from mainframes to personal computers with its flagship AutoCAD product. Today Autodesk operates as a subscription-based platform company. Unless otherwise noted, fiscal year references use Autodesk's January 31 fiscal year end: fiscal 2025 ("FY2025") refers to the year ended January 31, 2025, and fiscal 2026 ("FY2026") refers to the year ended January 31, 2026.

The product portfolio is organized into four families:

**Architecture, Engineering, Construction and Operations (AECO)** — The largest segment, generating $2.94 billion in FY2025 revenue (up 14% year-over-year), AECO includes the AEC Collection (AutoCAD, AutoCAD Civil 3D, Revit), Autodesk Construction Cloud (Autodesk Build, BIM Collaborate Pro, BuildingConnected), and Tandem for digital twins. Revit is the industry-standard Building Information Modeling (BIM) tool for architects and structural engineers. In May 2024, Autodesk acquired Payapps Limited, a cloud-based software platform for construction payment management, extending Autodesk Construction Cloud into the financial workflow layer of the project lifecycle. Autodesk Forma, the company's next-generation industry cloud, was launched in FY2024 to unify planning, design, construction, and operations workflows. As of March 24, 2026, Autodesk Construction Cloud is being consolidated into Autodesk Forma, signaling the company's strategic direction toward a fully integrated AECO industry cloud.

**AutoCAD and AutoCAD LT** — Contributing $1.57 billion in FY2025 revenue (up 8% year-over-year), AutoCAD remains the cornerstone drafting and design application across industries. AutoCAD is estimated to hold approximately 39% market share in the broader CAD software market, functioning as both a standalone tool and an extensible platform supporting thousands of third-party add-on applications.

**Manufacturing (MFG)** — At $1.19 billion in FY2025 revenue (up 12% year-over-year), the manufacturing segment includes Fusion (formerly Fusion 360), a cloud-native CAD/CAM/CAE platform; Inventor for 3D mechanical design; the Product Design and Manufacturing Collection; and Vault for data management. Fusion is the primary growth vehicle for converging design and manufacturing workflows on a single cloud-based platform.

**Media and Entertainment (M&E)** — The smallest segment at $315 million in FY2025 revenue (up 7% year-over-year), M&E includes Maya and 3ds Max for 3D modeling and animation, Flow Production Tracking for production management, and the M&E Collection. These tools are industry standards for film visual effects, game development, and design visualization.

For FY2026, Autodesk reported total revenue of $7.21 billion, up 18% year-over-year. The Q4 FY2026 earnings release presented results under a two-category segmentation: Design ($5.98 billion, up 17%) and Make ($796 million, up 22%), reflecting the company's strategic framing of workflow convergence across design and fabrication.

### Revenue Streams, Customer Segments, and Monetization

Autodesk generates substantially all of its revenue through subscription-based software. In FY2025, recurring revenue represented 97% of total net revenue of $6.13 billion, with subscription revenue growing 12% to $5.72 billion. Net revenue retention rate (NR3) was within the range of 100% to 110% on a constant currency basis as of January 31, 2025, indicating that existing customers are at minimum maintaining and generally expanding their spending.

The monetization model operates through several mechanisms:

- **Product subscriptions**: Term-based licenses combining desktop software and cloud functionality, representing the bulk of recurring revenue.
- **Enterprise Business Agreements (EBAs)**: Token-based access giving enterprise customers flexible use across the full product portfolio over a defined contract term.
- **Flex**: A pay-as-you-go consumption option allowing pre-purchased tokens for daily access to any Flex-eligible product.
- **Industry Collections**: Bundled product suites targeting specific verticals (AEC Collection, Product Design and Manufacturing Collection, M&E Collection), simplifying procurement for customers who need multiple tools.

Geographically, FY2025 revenue split as follows (per the company's 10-K): Americas $2.72 billion (44%), including U.S. $2.23 billion; EMEA $2.31 billion (38%); and APAC $1.11 billion (18%). On a constant currency basis, Americas grew 12%, EMEA 13%, and APAC 13%.

The company sells through a hybrid direct and indirect model. In FY2025, approximately 42% of revenue came from direct sales (up from 37% in FY2024), with 58% through approximately 1,260 resellers and distributors. The ongoing transition to Autodesk's new transaction model — where Solution Providers quote customers but transactions occur directly between Autodesk and the end customer — is shifting the channel mix toward direct relationships. Revenue through TD Synnex, the largest distributor, declined from 39% of total revenue in FY2024 to 33% in FY2025.

Customer segments span individual professionals and small businesses (reached through product subscriptions and the online Autodesk store) to large enterprises (served through EBAs and dedicated account teams). The company provides free educational licenses to students, educators, and academic institutions, seeding future professional adoption.

### Market Characteristics

**Retention and recurring revenue dynamics.** NR3 in the 100-110% range reflects stable-to-expanding revenue per existing customer. Remaining performance obligations (RPO) reached $8.30 billion at Q4 FY2026 (up 20% year-over-year), providing substantial forward revenue visibility. Current RPO — revenue expected within the next twelve months — stood at $5.48 billion at Q4 FY2026, up from $4.46 billion at the end of FY2025.

**Sales cycles and seasonality.** Enterprise deals, particularly EBAs, exhibit pronounced fourth-quarter seasonality. While this does not immediately impact recognized revenue (subscription revenue is recognized ratably over the contract term), it affects billings, RPO, and cash collections. Q4 FY2026 billings reached $2.80 billion, up 33% year-over-year. The transition to annual billings for multi-year contracts has further shifted the timing of billings and cash collections.

**Margins.** Autodesk's software model generates a gross margin of 92.3% (TTM). FY2025 GAAP operating income was $1.35 billion on $6.13 billion in revenue, an operating margin of approximately 22.1%. By FY2026, operating margin had improved to 27.1% on a trailing basis, reflecting subscription model operating leverage as the billing transition matured. Net profit margin was 15.6% on a TTM basis through FY2026. GAAP results include significant stock-based compensation expense ($683 million in FY2025, or approximately 11% of revenue), which materially widens the gap between GAAP and non-GAAP profitability figures; analysts typically examine both when assessing underlying economics. A separate consideration: the new transaction model reclassifies channel incentives from contra-revenue to operating expense, mechanically inflating reported revenue growth while compressing operating margin, with neutral impact to operating profit dollars and free cash flow.

**Free cash flow generation.** FY2026 free cash flow reached $2.41 billion (up 54% year-over-year per the Q4 FY2026 earnings release), reflecting the maturing subscription base and the completion of the billing model transition. The company repurchased $852 million of common stock in FY2025 per SEC filings and has maintained an active buyback program.

**Growth trajectory.** Autodesk guided FY2027 revenue of $8.10–$8.17 billion (approximately 12–13% growth) with free cash flow of $2.70–$2.80 billion, citing momentum in AECO, construction, and continued sales optimization.

### Sources of Competitive Advantage

**Switching costs.** AutoCAD and Revit are deeply embedded in professional workflows across architecture, engineering, and construction. Projects, templates, custom configurations, third-party plug-ins, and accumulated training are all built around Autodesk file formats and data models. Migrating away from these tools requires retraining entire teams, converting legacy project files, and rebuilding established processes — costs that most firms find prohibitive. NR3 in the 100-110% range reflects this stickiness.

**Industry-standard status and ecosystem lock-in.** AutoCAD, developed over four decades, has established its file formats (DWG, RVT) as de facto standards in many industries. Autodesk's API-based architecture facilitates thousands of third-party add-on applications. This ecosystem creates a self-reinforcing cycle: more users attract more developers, which increases the software's utility and draws additional users.

**Network effects in collaboration platforms.** Autodesk Construction Cloud and BIM Collaborate Pro enable multi-party collaboration across project teams — architects, engineers, general contractors, and subcontractors. The value of these platforms increases as more participants in a project ecosystem adopt them, favoring the incumbent. BuildingConnected's preconstruction network, connecting general contractors with subcontractors for bid management, exhibits similar dynamics. The Payapps acquisition further extends this network into construction payment workflows, deepening the platform's transactional reach.

**Breadth of portfolio.** Autodesk is one of the few companies offering end-to-end solutions spanning early-stage design (Forma) through detailed engineering (Revit, Inventor), construction management (Autodesk Build), manufacturing (Fusion), and operations (Tandem). This breadth enables cross-sell and upsell across the project lifecycle, as reflected in the growth of Industry Collections and EBAs. Competitors tend to concentrate in narrower niches: Bentley Systems in infrastructure, PTC in discrete manufacturing, Dassault Systèmes in high-end PLM.

**Cloud and AI platform investment.** Autodesk has invested in cloud-native architecture for over a decade, with Fusion and Autodesk Construction Cloud as flagship cloud products. The company is integrating AI capabilities — including generative design, automated suggestions, and AI-powered analysis — across its platform, leveraging proprietary geometry kernels and domain-specific data models. The $200 million strategic investment in World Labs (February 2026) to bring world models into 3D workflows illustrates the company's commitment to AI-driven platform expansion.

**Intellectual property.** Autodesk maintains an active patent portfolio covering CAD, BIM, simulation, and cloud collaboration technologies. Its accumulated proprietary technology — particularly in geometry kernels, data models, and domain-specific compute — represents meaningful know-how that would take significant time for new entrants to replicate.

**Distribution network.** A global network of approximately 1,260 resellers and distributors provides deep reach into volume markets worldwide. Combined with the ongoing shift to direct customer relationships through the new transaction model, Autodesk has both broad market coverage and increasingly direct access to end-user data and buying relationships.


## Competitive Landscape

Autodesk operates across several overlapping software markets — architecture, engineering, construction and operations (AECO); manufacturing and product design; and media and entertainment — each with distinct competitive dynamics. The company's 10-K identifies a broad competitive field that includes "large, global, publicly traded companies; small, geographically focused firms; startup firms; and solutions produced in-house by their users." In practice, competition varies by end market.

### Direct Competitors

**Dassault Systèmes SE (Euronext Paris: DSY)** is Autodesk's closest global rival. Through CATIA and SolidWorks, Dassault competes across CAD and product lifecycle management (PLM), with particular strength in high-end aerospace and automotive simulation through its 3DEXPERIENCE platform. Dassault maintains deeper PLM integration than Autodesk in these verticals but has less penetration in the AEC sector, where Autodesk dominates through Revit and its construction platform.

**PTC Inc. (Nasdaq: PTC)** competes directly in manufacturing and product design with its Creo CAD platform and Windchill PLM software. At $2.9 billion in trailing revenue and an $18.6 billion market cap, PTC is substantially smaller than Autodesk but has carved out a strong position in discrete manufacturing. PTC reported 21.4% year-over-year revenue growth, partly driven by its augmented reality platform Vuforia and an accelerating SaaS transition. PTC's gross margin of 84.2% and operating margin of 33.8% reflect a profitable but narrower business.

**Siemens Digital Industries Software, a division of Siemens AG (Xetra: SIE)**, competes across manufacturing, automotive, and industrial IoT through its NX and Solid Edge products. Backed by Siemens AG's industrial scale, it benefits from hardware-software integration that Autodesk cannot replicate. Siemens targets large industrial enterprises and is pushing cloud-enabled CAD with AI features. Its scale and parent company resources make it a formidable competitor, though higher complexity and cost limit penetration among small and mid-size enterprises.

**Bentley Systems, Incorporated (Nasdaq: BSY)** focuses specifically on infrastructure engineering with its iTwin digital twin platform and MicroStation. Bentley competes most directly with Autodesk in civil infrastructure and public works, but has a narrower portfolio and weaker presence in general manufacturing and building design.

### Adjacent Competitors

**Adobe Inc. (Nasdaq: ADBE)** is listed by Autodesk as a primary competitor in its 10-K, though the overlap is mainly in design visualization and digital content creation rather than core engineering. Adobe's $109.8 billion market cap and $23.8 billion in trailing revenue dwarf Autodesk, and the two companies serve largely different customer workflows.

**Procore Technologies, Inc. (NYSE: PCOR)** competes in construction management, overlapping with Autodesk's construction platform. Autodesk's broader product suite and ability to connect design-to-construction workflows gives it an integration advantage against Procore's pure-play focus. Autodesk is further consolidating this advantage: Autodesk Construction Cloud is scheduled to merge into Autodesk Forma as of March 24, 2026, unifying its construction and design management capabilities under a single platform and strengthening its integrated value proposition against pure-play competitors.

**Trimble Inc. (Nasdaq: TRMB)** and **Hexagon AB (Nasdaq Stockholm: HEXA B)** both compete at the intersection of hardware sensors and design software. Hexagon's metrology software targets quality control in manufacturing, while Trimble competes in geospatial and construction. Both pose competitive pressure in niche segments but lack Autodesk's breadth.

**Nemetschek SE (Xetra: NEM)** competes in European AEC markets through subsidiaries including Graphisoft (ArchiCAD) and Bluebeam. Its geographic concentration and smaller scale limit its competitive threat in North America and Asia.

### Emerging Threats

The 10-K notes that "the software industry has limited barriers to entry" and that cloud and mobile computing have further lowered those barriers. Open-source CAD alternatives and cloud-native startups represent an emerging category of competition, particularly for price-sensitive small businesses; ActCAD offers one such lower-cost AutoCAD alternative. The rise of AI-driven generative design tools could create new competitive dynamics, though Autodesk's own investments in AI — including a $200 million stake in World Labs and a strategic investment in Toolpath for AI-driven manufacturing — position the company to lead rather than follow this transition.

### Competitive Position

Autodesk's primary competitive advantages are scale, switching costs, and portfolio breadth. The company holds approximately 42% of the 3D CAD market (per competitive_analysis.md), a position built over decades of AutoCAD and Revit penetration across architecture, engineering, and construction workflows. These products are deeply embedded in customer processes, creating high switching costs that support subscription renewals and pricing power.

The FY2025 10-K (ended January 31, 2025) reported 97% recurring revenue as a percentage of net revenue; the corresponding FY2026 figure will be available in the upcoming annual filing. This recurring revenue base provides visibility and stability that most competitors have not yet achieved.

The company's main competitive vulnerability is the ongoing restructuring of its customer-facing sales operations. Management flagged in the Q4 FY2026 (ended January 31, 2026) earnings call that "the probability and the potential impact of disruption are higher given the focus of the restructuring this year is on customer-facing sales functions." A $100 million restructuring charge was taken in Q4, with an additional $135–160 million in cash outflows expected in FY2027. If this transition disrupts customer relationships, competitors could exploit the window to gain share.

In slower-growing segments, Autodesk faces asymmetric risk. Media and entertainment revenue grew just 5% to $332 million in FY2026 (ended January 31, 2026, per Q4 FY2026 earnings release), and this segment faces competitive pressure from both commercial rivals (Adobe, SideFX's Houdini) and open-source tools like Blender. Conversely, Autodesk's AECO segment — which grew 22% to $3.6 billion (per Q4 FY2026 earnings release) — benefits from limited direct competition at scale and strong secular tailwinds from infrastructure spending and BIM adoption mandates.


## Supply Chain Positioning

Autodesk, Inc. (NASDAQ: ADSK) operates as a pure-play software company with no physical manufacturing, which fundamentally distinguishes its supply chain from hardware or materials-based businesses. Upstream dependencies center on cloud infrastructure and technology licensing; downstream distribution blends an established indirect channel with a growing direct-to-customer model.

### Upstream: Suppliers and Technology Dependencies

Autodesk's most material upstream dependency is cloud infrastructure. For its cloud-based products, the company relies on co-located hosting facilities and, increasingly, Amazon Web Services along with other infrastructure-as-a-service providers, as disclosed in its fiscal year 2025 (ended January 31, 2025) 10-K. This reliance on third-party cloud platforms introduces concentration risk: disruptions or pricing changes at major hyperscalers could affect both service delivery and gross margins. The 10-K identifies growth in cloud and AI workloads as a driver of cost of revenue increases going forward.

Software delivery has shifted almost entirely to electronic distribution. Customers receive downloads from third-party secure servers, though physical media remains available from multiple sources. This eliminates traditional manufacturing supply chain risk.

On the development side, Autodesk performs the majority of its R&D internally, with primary operations in the United States, Canada, and India; localization and translation are handled principally in Singapore and Ireland. The company also uses independent contractors for some product development and licenses third-party intellectual property under arrangements that may require royalty payments. None of these supplier relationships appear to represent single-source dependencies based on available disclosures.

### Downstream: Distribution and Customer Channels

Autodesk's go-to-market structure has been undergoing a significant transformation. Historically, the company relied on both a two-tiered indirect model (distributors to resellers) and a one-tiered model (Autodesk direct to resellers). As of January 31, 2025, the network included approximately 1,260 resellers and distributors worldwide. In fiscal 2025 (ended January 31, 2025), approximately 58% of revenue was derived from indirect channel sales.

The most notable distribution concentration is TD Synnex Corporation and its global affiliates, which accounted for 33% of net revenue in fiscal 2025 — approximately $2.0 billion — down from 39% in fiscal 2024 (ended January 31, 2024) and 37% in fiscal 2023 (ended January 31, 2023). This single-distributor dependency is substantial by any measure. However, Autodesk has been actively reducing this reliance through its new transaction model, under which Solution Providers provide quotes to customers but the actual transaction occurs directly between Autodesk and the end customer. This model rolled out across major markets during fiscal 2025. Autodesk also entered a narrower distribution agreement with TD Synnex focused on government business in certain jurisdictions and emerging markets, replacing the prior broad commercial distribution arrangement.

Direct channels include the online branded store, enterprise sales to named accounts, and the new transaction model. No single end customer accounted for 10% or more of revenue in fiscal 2025, indicating low customer concentration risk.

**Geographic exposure:** International sales constitute approximately 64% of total net revenue. By segment in fiscal 2025, Americas contributed $2.7 billion (44% of total), EMEA contributed $2.3 billion (38%), and APAC contributed $1.1 billion (18%). The company generates a significant portion of revenue from five markets: the United States, Germany, Japan, the United Kingdom, and Canada. This international footprint exposes Autodesk to currency fluctuation and regional economic conditions, particularly in Europe and Japan where local currency volatility can meaningfully affect reported results.

### Assessment

Autodesk's supply chain profile carries the advantages and limitations typical of a large-cap software company. Upstream, the absence of physical manufacturing eliminates raw material and logistics risk, but introduces meaningful dependency on cloud infrastructure providers whose pricing and reliability directly affect margins and service quality.

Downstream, the company is mid-transition in a multi-year channel overhaul that is reducing distributor concentration — particularly the outsized TD Synnex relationship — while building more direct customer relationships. The new transaction model also shifts how channel incentives are classified (from contra-revenue to operating expenses), which management expects to positively affect reported revenue growth while being broadly neutral to operating profit and free cash flow dollars.

A structural feature worth noting: Autodesk's subscription-based annual billing model generates large upfront deferred revenue ($3.79 billion current deferred revenue as of January 31, 2025), resulting in persistently negative working capital (-$1.67 billion). This is not a liquidity concern — it reflects the cash-positive nature of the subscription model, where customers prepay before Autodesk recognizes the revenue. This dynamic is the inverse of a typical product supply chain and represents a meaningful cash flow advantage.

Execution risk in the channel transition is real. The 10-K discloses that Autodesk initiated a restructuring plan during fiscal 2026 (ended January 31, 2026) expected to generate cost savings across both marketing and sales and research and development functions in fiscal 2027. The scope of the restructuring extends beyond channel activities and reflects a broad organizational realignment accompanying the go-to-market transformation.


## 6. Financial and Operating Leverage

Autodesk's financial profile reflects a capital-light subscription model: exceptional gross margins, growing free cash flow, moderate leverage, and disciplined capital return to shareholders.

### Financial Leverage

As of January 31, 2025, Autodesk had $2.30 billion in aggregate principal amount of senior notes outstanding, plus $272 million in capital lease obligations, for total debt of approximately $2.56 billion. Against this, the company held $2.15 billion in cash, cash equivalents, and marketable securities, producing net debt of approximately $688 million—down from $1.1 billion three years prior.

The debt maturity profile is well-staggered. Per the 10-K contractual obligations table, notes payable (including interest) total $2.58 billion, with $361 million due in fiscal 2026, $601 million across fiscal 2027–2028, $576 million across fiscal 2029–2030, and $1.05 billion thereafter. No amounts were drawn on the company's $1.5 billion revolving credit facility (expandable up to $2.0 billion) as of January 31, 2025 or the March 2025 filing date. The revolver matures September 30, 2026—seven months from the analysis date—meaning refinancing or extension will be required in the near term. Absent a new agreement, the company's primary uncommitted liquidity backstop will expire.

The balance sheet shows a debt-to-equity ratio of 89.8% (per key ratios data), elevated relative to peers such as Adobe (57.3%), PTC (35.9%), Synopsys (35.6%), and Cadence (48.7%). However, this ratio reflects Autodesk's accumulated deficit of $1.33 billion—a legacy of its subscription transition and aggressive buyback program—rather than an outsized debt burden. Total stockholders' equity has improved steadily, from $849 million in fiscal 2022 to $2.62 billion in fiscal 2025.

In fiscal 2025, net interest and investment income was $34 million (positive), compared to net interest expense of $71 million just two years earlier. With $1.55 billion in EBITDA (fiscal 2025) against modest interest obligations, the company maintains comfortable debt service coverage.

Approximately 66% of cash and marketable securities are held in foreign jurisdictions. Management has stated that foreign earnings are generally available for repatriation with minimal incremental U.S. tax impact.

### Operating Leverage

Autodesk's software business exhibits pronounced operating leverage, a defining characteristic of subscription-based models with high fixed costs and minimal variable costs per incremental user.

**Cost structure.** Gross margin was 92.3% on a trailing twelve-month basis, among the highest of its peer group and ahead of Adobe (89.3%), PTC (84.2%), Synopsys (82.0%), and Cadence (86.4%). On a fiscal 2025 GAAP basis, cost of revenue totaled $578 million on $6.13 billion in revenue, equating to a 90.6% gross margin, and consisting primarily of cloud hosting, product support labor, royalties, and depreciation. This cost base is predominantly fixed or semi-fixed in nature; it does not scale linearly with revenue growth.

**Margin expansion mechanics.** Revenue grew 12% in fiscal 2025 while total cost of revenue increased 13% and operating expenses grew 9%, yielding GAAP operating margin improvement from 20.5% in fiscal 2024 to 22.1% in fiscal 2025. On a non-GAAP basis, operating margin held near 36% across fiscal 2023, 2024, and 2025—flat rather than expanding, as the company reinvested subscription revenue gains into R&D ($1.49 billion, up 8%) and sales and marketing ($2.0 billion, up 10%). The company guided FY2027 revenue to $8.10–$8.17 billion, non-GAAP EPS to $12.29–$12.56, and free cash flow to $2.70–$2.80 billion.

**Sensitivity to revenue changes.** The high fixed-cost base means that incremental revenue drops almost entirely to operating profit after the gross margin threshold. R&D and G&A expenses, at $1.49 billion and $650 million respectively, are largely headcount-driven and adjust slowly. In a downturn, the same operating leverage that amplifies margin expansion would magnify margin contraction. The subscription model provides some buffer: 97% of fiscal 2025 revenue was recurring, and net revenue retention remained in the 100–110% range on a constant-currency basis.

**Scalability indicators.** Revenue per diluted share increased from $20.00 in fiscal 2022 to $28.25 in fiscal 2025, and $33.83 in the TTM period through fiscal 2026, reflecting both top-line growth and share reduction. Remaining performance obligations of $6.94 billion at fiscal year-end 2025 (up 14%) and $8.30 billion at FY2026 year-end provide forward visibility. The transition to a new direct transaction model and annual billing is further improving revenue predictability, though it has created near-term noise in reported growth rates.

### Cash Flow Generation and Working Capital

Autodesk delivered its strongest cash flow year on record in fiscal 2026: full-year revenue reached $7.21 billion (up 18% year-over-year) and free cash flow grew to $2.41 billion (up 54%), representing an FCF margin of approximately 33%. Fiscal 2025 operating cash flow was $1.61 billion, up from $1.31 billion in fiscal 2024, with the prior year's weakness reflecting the transition away from multi-year upfront billing.

Working capital is structurally negative, typical of subscription software companies. ### Capital Allocation

Autodesk allocates capital across three primary channels: share repurchases, acquisitions, and organic reinvestment. The company pays no dividend.

**Share repurchases.** Buybacks are the primary return mechanism. The company repurchased $858 million of stock in fiscal 2025 (3 million shares) and $795 million in fiscal 2024. In November 2024, the board authorized an additional $5.0 billion in repurchase capacity on top of $3.88 billion remaining from prior programs, creating total authorization of $8.88 billion. This signals management's intent to continue reducing the share count, which declined from 218 million shares (fiscal 2022) to 214 million (fiscal 2025). At current prices, the outstanding authorization represents roughly 17% of the company's market capitalization.

**Acquisitions.** Autodesk spent $825 million on acquisitions in fiscal 2025 (including Payapps for construction payment management), compared to $70 million in fiscal 2024 and $96 million in fiscal 2023. The company has historically pursued tuck-in acquisitions to extend its platform—a strategy reflected in $4.24 billion of goodwill on the balance sheet. More recently, the $200 million strategic investment in World Labs (February 2026) targets AI capabilities for 3D design workflows.

**Organic reinvestment.** R&D spending reached $1.49 billion in fiscal 2025 (24.2% of revenue), up from $1.12 billion in fiscal 2022 (25.4% of revenue). The declining R&D intensity ratio alongside rising absolute spending illustrates the operating leverage inherent in the model. The company is directing investment toward cloud platform capabilities, AI integration, and the Fusion/Forma/Flow product family.

**Prioritization.** The absence of a dividend, combined with substantial buyback authorization and modest acquisition activity, indicates management prioritizes share count reduction and organic growth over income distribution. With FY2027 guided free cash flow of $2.70–$2.80 billion (discussed above), the company has ample capacity to fund both buybacks and selective acquisitions without increasing leverage.

A discounted cash flow analysis anchored to management's FY2027 free cash flow guidance provides a useful reference. Key inputs:

- **Margin trajectory**: Non-GAAP operating margin guided to expand approximately 75 basis points in FY2027 from the ~38% Q4 FY2026 level; management targets further expansion from cloud scale and operating leverage

Traditional book value metrics offer limited insight for a capital-light software company.
